No constituency in Chennai is all good or all bad. Mylapore — ancient, dense, proud — is a study in contrasts. It leads the city in civic spirit, health access, and school staffing. It also floods every monsoon, taxes its residents heavily and returns less than it should, and is slowly being hollowed out by demographic change. Here are ten of each.

🏛
A note on scope

Mylapore is rightly celebrated for the Kapaleeshwarar temple, its December music sabhas, the Margazhi season, its ancient temples, theerthams, and cultural traditions that make it the spiritual heart of Chennai. None of that is in this report — and intentionally so. This is a civic data report: it covers health, education, flooding, transport, and governance — the metrics that affect daily quality of life and can be measured, compared, and demanded from elected representatives. Mylapore's cultural heritage is its soul. This report is about its roads, drains, and schools.

✦ Highlights
10 things Mylapore gets right
H1
🗳
Highest voter turnout in all of Chennai
71.6%
vs city average of 63.4%

In the 2021 TN Assembly elections, Mylapore recorded the highest turnout of all 18 constituencies. This is not a fluke — it reflects a deeply rooted culture of civic participation that spans generations and makes this constituency a model for democratic engagement.

SourceTN Assembly Elections 2021 (OpenCity.in) · Civic score: 75/100 — Rank 1 in Chennai
H2
🏥
Five Urban Primary Health Centres (Urban Primary Health Centre (UPHC)s) — best health access ratio in South Chennai
5
Urban Primary Health Centres · 1 per ~38,000 residents

With 5 UPHCs for 191,400 voters, Mylapore is among the top 4 constituencies citywide on health centre density. Compare to Royapuram and Sholinganallur — each with just 2 UPHCs for larger populations.

SourceGreater Chennai Corporation (GCC) UPHCs & Urban Community Health Centres (Urban Community Health Centre (UCHC)s) map (OpenCity.in) · Health score: 79/100
H3
📚
Best pupil-teacher ratio — tied with Anna Nagar at top
23:1
Pupil-Teacher Ratio (PTR) in govt schools · WHO recommends ≤ 30:1

Children in Mylapore's government schools get far more individual teacher attention than anywhere else in the city. The worst in Chennai — Sholinganallur at 44:1 — means kids there get nearly half the teacher time per student.

SourceUnified District Information System for Education Plus (Unified District Information System for Education (UDISE)+) 2023-24 · Education score: 81/100 · Rank 3 overall
H4
🚌
35 Metropolitan Transport Corporation (MTC) bus stops — second most in Chennai after Egmore
35
Bus shelters · City median: 24 · Plus metro access

Combined with metro access via Alwarpet and Teynampet stations and Mass Rapid Transit System (MRTS) along the coast, Mylapore residents have genuine multi-modal transport options. Only 9 of 18 constituencies have metro access at all.

SourceChennai Bus Shelters dataset · Metro Monthly Usage · Transport score: 81/100
H5
🌿
Adyar Estuary & temple tanks — a rare urban biodiversity asset

The Adyar River and estuary along Mylapore's southern edge is one of Chennai's most ecologically significant wetland systems. Multiple temple tanks — Kapali, Kesava Perumal — act as groundwater recharge points. The City Biodiversity Index designates this corridor as ecologically sensitive and a natural flood buffer.

SourceChennai City Biodiversity Index · Waterbodies dataset · Master Plan 2026 (OpenCity.in)
H6
🏛
Strongest resident voice infrastructure — active Resident Welfare Associations (Resident Welfare Association (RWA)s) & citizen manifestos

Mylapore has multiple functioning RWAs and the Mylapore Times — a neighbourhood community news platform covering civic issues. Formal citizen manifestos have been submitted ahead of the 2026 elections, making this one of the most organised constituencies for accountability in the entire city.

SourceCitizen Manifestos for TN Elections 2026 · Ward Committee records (OpenCity.in)
H7
💧
More consistent water supply than North Chennai peers

Zone-wise water supply data shows South Chennai, including Mylapore, receives more consistent piped water than North Chennai constituencies like Thiruvottiyur and Royapuram which face chronic shortages. Chennai Metropolitan Water Supply and Sewerage Board (CMWSSB)'s distribution network is more mature and reliable in this zone.

SourceChennai Water Supply dataset · CMWSSB GIS · Ward-wise Groundwater Levels (OpenCity.in)
H8
🏪
High commercial density drives one of the stronger ward tax bases

Luz Church Road, San Thome High Road, and R.A. Puram make Mylapore's wards among the higher property-tax contributing zones in South Chennai. This means the constituency funds more GCC services than most peers — making its infrastructure return gap (see L8) all the more unjustifiable.

SourceProperty Tax Collections (ward-wise till 2017-18) · GCC Budget 2025-26 (OpenCity.in)
H9
🌳
Above-average parks & open space for a dense urban constituency

Nageswara Rao Park, the Marina Green, and multiple neighbourhood open spaces give Mylapore a green per-capita ratio higher than comparable dense areas like Royapuram or Perambur. These spaces serve as informal community hubs and critical urban cooling islands.

SourceChennai Parks dataset · Master Plan 2026 open space designations (OpenCity.in)
H10
🚇
Metro access gives a long-term transit dividend as the network expands

Only 9 of 18 constituencies have metro access today. Mylapore is one. As Chennai Metro Rail Limited (CMRL) Phase 2 expands the network southward, Mylapore's connectivity advantage will compound — a structural asset for residents, businesses, and property values that most of Chennai cannot yet claim.

SourceChennai Metro Monthly Usage · MRTS Data · Comprehensive Mobility Plan (OpenCity.in)

Mylapore leads Chennai in civic voice. Its residents vote the most, organise the best, and file the most detailed manifestos. The tragedy is that this civic capital has not yet been converted into proportionate infrastructure quality.

Lowlights
✦ Lowlights
10 things Mylapore needs to fix
L1
🌊
Flood safety score of 73 — the single gap preventing Rank 1
−10
Points behind Anna Nagar on flood safety (73 vs 83)

Buckingham Canal, R.A. Puram pockets, and low-lying lanes near the coast flood every northeast monsoon. This 10-point flood deficit is mathematically what keeps Mylapore at Rank 2. Fix the drains, fix the rank.

SourceChennai Flooding Inundation dataset · Floods 2015 & 2020 data (OpenCity.in)
L2
⚡
Sewerage network at capacity — floods mixed with sewage in 2020

The Sewerage Capacity dataset shows the Mylapore–Mandaveli zone operates near saturation during peak monsoon. In 2020, primary sewers and the Buckingham Canal overflowed simultaneously — a public health emergency. A proposed relief sewer for Mandaveli–Santhome remains unfunded in the GCC Budget 2025-26.

SourceChennai Sewerage Capacity map · Sewage Collection System KML · Floods 2020 data (OpenCity.in)
L3
📉
Shrinking electorate — 5th smallest in Chennai, demographic hollowing
1.91L
Voters · Below city median of 2.14L · Stagnant while others grow

Rising property prices are pushing young families to the suburbs, slowly hollowing out Mylapore's working-age population. While Velachery has 2.67L voters and Sholinganallur 2.54L and growing, Mylapore's voter roll is static.

SourceVoter Rolls Jan 2025 · NSS Multi-Indicator Survey 2020-21 (OpenCity.in)
L4
🚶
Poor last-mile to metro — especially for women & elderly after 9 PM

Despite metro access, pedestrian infrastructure between Mylapore's interior lanes and Alwarpet/Teynampet stations is inadequate — broken footpaths, no shelter, poor lighting. Feeder bus frequency collapses after 9 PM, a critical gap for women commuters and the elderly.

SourceChennai Comprehensive Mobility Plan · MTC Performance Data · Bus Shelters dataset (OpenCity.in)
L5
💸
Pays high property taxes — infrastructure return lags contribution

Mylapore wards are among South Chennai's highest GCC tax contributors. Yet residents consistently report that road resurfacing, drain desilting, and footpath quality are below what their contribution should deliver. This fiscal mismatch is unquantified and rarely challenged in ward committee meetings.

SourceChennai Property Tax Collections (till 2017-18) · GCC Budget 2025-26 (OpenCity.in)
L6
🌡
Rising urban heat island — high density, declining tree cover

Land Surface Temperature (LST) data for May–June 2025 shows Mylapore's dense zones recording surface temperatures 3–5°C above the city's greener areas. Normalised Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) data confirms declining tree cover in Mandaveli and R.A. Puram as buildings replace garden plots. Urban heat stress is a growing but invisible health hazard.

SourceLST Geotiff May–Jun 2025 · NDVI 2025 · Normalised Difference Built-up Index (NDBI) 2025 Built-up Index (Data Jam datasets)
L7
🏫
School facility quality lags — labs, digital access & toilets are gaps

A good PTR of 23:1 masks physical infrastructure shortcomings. UDISE+ data flags gaps in science lab availability, digital classroom access, and functional toilet ratios in Mylapore's government schools. Good teacher staffing alone does not equal quality education.

SourceUDISE+ 2023-24 · UDISE+ 2021-22 · Chennai Schools map (OpenCity.in)
L8
⚠️
Adyar Estuary under threat — sewage & encroachment degrading a heritage asset

The same estuary cited as Highlight H5 is under active threat. Sewage discharge, garbage dumping, and buffer zone encroachment are documented in the Waterbodies Census. Without intervention, this ecological asset will deteriorate into a liability — worsening both floods and air quality.

SourceTN Waterbodies Census · Chennai Waterbodies dataset · Sewage Pumping Network (OpenCity.in)
L9
🗺
Solid waste blind spots near the temple corridor and Luz market

The Solid Waste Management (SWM) dataset shows bin density and biogas centre coverage drops along the Kapaleeshwarar temple corridor and Luz market — highest footfall zones in the constituency. Street-level waste accumulation in these pockets directly contradicts Mylapore's civic reputation and is a recurring RWA complaint.

SourceChennai Solid Waste Management Data — bins, biogas centres, landfills (OpenCity.in)
L10
📣
Manifestos filed for 2026 — but MLA accountability is unverified

Mylapore RWAs have filed detailed citizen manifestos covering floods, roads, the waterfront, and schools. But how many of these have been formally raised on the Assembly floor by the sitting MLA? The MLA Questions dataset tracks exactly this — and no one has cross-referenced it yet. Participation without accountability is incomplete democracy.

SourceCitizen Manifestos for TN Elections 2026 · MLA Questions dataset (OpenCity.in)
✦ The Verdict

10 vs 10 — and the Score Says It All

Mylapore's highlights and lowlights are not opposites — they are the same neighbourhood seen from two angles. The high civic engagement (H1) makes L10's accountability gap all the more urgent. The biodiversity asset (H5) makes L8's estuary degradation all the more tragic. The strong tax base (H8) makes L5's infrastructure deficit all the more unjustifiable.

The path to Rank 1 is mathematically clear: solve L1 (flood safety) and L2 (sewerage), and Mylapore's overall score crosses 82 — enough to surpass Anna Nagar. Everything else — urban heat, last-mile gaps, school facilities — is the longer work that a genuinely engaged constituency is uniquely positioned to demand.

The question for 2026 is not whether Mylapore knows its problems. Its residents clearly do. The question is whether that knowledge becomes organised pressure on the people responsible for fixing it.

✦ 2026 Tamil Nadu Assembly Elections

Ask Your Candidate

One month to elections. These are the asks citizens of Mylapore should put to every candidate who knocks on their door — grouped by who needs what most.

⏳  ~30 days to polling day · Use your vote as a question

Mylapore contains within it two very different worlds. The fishing hamlets of Nochikuppam, Santhome, and Foreshore Estate — home to over 1,500 families — exist alongside the affluent streets of Alwarpet. Slum clearance board resettlements in Muthaiyapuram, Ambedkar Bridge, and Pallakku Manager have displaced hundreds more. These are their asks. Backed by reporting from Citizen Matters, The Wire, The News Minute, and ground-level documentation.

1
Restore the Nochikuppam fishing community's right to their traditional livelihood space
Over 1,500 fisher families in Nochikuppam have faced repeated eviction from the Marina Loop Road market under the pretext of beautification. Fisherwomen — the primary breadwinners — have lost their seashore stalls. The new market has poor footfall and unfair stall allotments. Ask: Will you fight for in-situ livelihood spaces for fishing communities, and oppose displacement without consent?
Most urgent Livelihood
2
Complete the pending Tamil Nadu Slum Clearance Board (TNSCB) housing for displaced tsunami & flood-affected families
Only 628 of 7,320 originally planned TNSCB tenements were built after the 2004 tsunami. Around 400 families in Nochikuppam still live in temporary structures over two decades later. Ask: What is your specific timeline and budget commitment to completing housing for all displaced families in Muthaiyapuram, Visalatchi Thottam, and Nochikuppam?
Long overdue Housing
3
Provide patta (land title deeds) to long-settled fisher families in coastal hamlets
Residents of Nochikuppam have lived on their land for over 60 years, yet most have no title deeds. Without pattas, they cannot access bank loans, legal protections, or compensation. The Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) Notification recognises their rights — but the state has not acted. Ask: Will you move a resolution in the Assembly to regularise and document land rights for all Mylapore coastal settlements?
Land rights
4
Free up GCC health sub-centres for evening hours and add a mobile health van for kuppam areas
Despite 5 UPHCs in Mylapore, fishing hamlets and slum pockets have poor access due to distance and working hours. Fisherwomen who return from work by afternoon cannot access health centres that close at 4 PM. Ask: Will you ensure at least one UPHC near Nochikuppam-Foreshore Estate has extended evening hours and a monthly mobile health camp?
Healthcare
5
Build a proper bus terminus and last-mile connectivity for Foreshore Estate residents
The sitting MLA promised a new bus terminus in 2021 but delayed it citing Metro Rail works. Foreshore Estate and coastal settlement residents depend entirely on MTC and share autos. Ask: What is the committed opening date for the Mylapore bus terminus, and will you add feeder routes directly connecting kuppam areas to metro stations?
Transport
Also affects low income residents
+
Desilt Buckingham Canal before every monsoon — with a publicly verifiable schedule
Buckingham Canal flooding hits the most vulnerable hardest. Low-lying slum pockets flood first and drain last. Residents in Ambedkar Bridge area have been relocated at least once due to flooding. Ask: Will you commit to a pre-monsoon desilting calendar published online, with ward-level completion certificates?
Urgent Flood

The middle-income residents of Mylapore — apartment dwellers in Mandaveli, R.A. Puram, and Luz, shopkeepers on San Thome High Road, teachers and nurses, autorickshaw drivers and small traders — face a different set of daily frustrations. These are the people most directly disrupted by Metro Rail construction, interior road neglect, and post-flood disruption to livelihoods. Real issues from DT Next, Citizen Matters, and resident forums.

1
Set a hard deadline for Metro Rail Phase 2 construction disruption — and compensate affected businesses
Metro construction has closed several arterial roads in Mylapore causing severe traffic snarls. Small traders on Luz Church Road and shops near construction zones report 30–50% drop in footfall. Ask: What is the exact completion date for Metro Rail works in Mylapore, and will you push for a business disruption compensation fund for affected shopkeepers?
Right now Metro
2
Fix interior roads — the main roads are repaired but lanes in Mandaveli, Luz, and R.A. Puram interior remain broken
Ganesh, an autorickshaw driver quoted in DT Next, summed it up clearly: "While main roads are maintained, several interior roads remain underdeveloped." This is a constant complaint in ward forums. Ask: Can you publish a lane-by-lane road repair schedule for Mylapore's interior streets with completion timelines?
Roads
3
Clear temple corridor encroachments — shops are blocking pathways and causing safety hazards
Encroachments by shops around Kapaleeshwarar temple have become a major obstruction for residents and devotees, according to residents quoted in DT Next coverage. The issue has been raised repeatedly without action. Ask: Will you initiate a formal encroachment audit around the temple corridor and give a 90-day clearance commitment with GCC enforcement?
Civic Order
4
Upgrade government school infrastructure — digital classrooms, functional labs, and clean toilets
Middle-income parents who send children to government schools (or who consider it) care about facility quality. PTR at 23:1 is excellent, but UDISE data flags gaps in labs and digital access. Ask: Will you commit GCC Budget funds specifically for school facility upgrades in all government schools in Mylapore — with independent UDISE-verified reporting?
Education
5
Improve women's safety on the last-mile walk to metro — better lighting, footpaths, and feeder buses after 9 PM
Women commuters from Mylapore's interior lanes to Alwarpet and Teynampet metro stations face broken footpaths, poor lighting, and no feeder buses after 9 PM. This is the most frequently raised transit issue in community forums. Ask: Will you take up with CMRL and MTC the specific ask of 10 PM feeder frequency on Mylapore metro-connecting routes, and a lit pedestrian path audit?
Women's safety Transport

Mylapore's higher-income residents — professionals in Alwarpet and Raja Annamalaipuram, property owners in R.A. Puram, heritage homeowners, and the significant Non-Resident Indian (NRI) community with roots in the constituency — tend to engage through RWAs rather than at street level. Their asks are about governance quality, long-term asset protection, and environmental stewardship. Many are the same people filing citizen manifestos ahead of 2026.

1
Publish a ward-wise breakdown of how Mylapore's property tax contribution is spent by GCC
Mylapore wards are among South Chennai's highest property tax contributors. Yet infrastructure quality — footpaths, drain maintenance, road surfacing — is widely felt to lag. Ask: Will you file an Right to Information (RTI) or Assembly question demanding ward-by-ward expenditure data for Mylapore, and publish it openly on the constituency website?
Fiscal accountability
2
Fund and expedite the Mandaveli–Santhome relief sewer before the 2026 monsoon
The 2020 floods produced a documented sewage-flood mix emergency in Mylapore. CMWSSB proposed a relief sewer for the Mandaveli–Santhome stretch but it remains unfunded in GCC Budget 2025-26. Ask: Will you specifically push for this sewer project to be included in the revised GCC budget with a committed construction start date before July 2026?
Pre-monsoon 2026 Sewerage
3
Restore the Adyar Estuary — stop sewage discharge, remove encroachments, and gazette a buffer zone
The Adyar Estuary and Buckingham Canal are degraded by sewage discharge and garbage dumping documented in the Waterbodies Census. The Chennai Master Plan 2026 designates parts as conservation zones — but enforcement is absent. Ask: Will you move a motion in the Assembly to formally gazette a 50-metre no-encroachment buffer along the Adyar in Mylapore, with CMWSSB enforcement?
Environment Heritage
4
Launch a Mylapore Heritage Street programme — pedestrianisation of select lanes near the temple
Several streets near Kapaleeshwarar temple and the Mylapore tank are natural candidates for pedestrian priority — a move that would reduce encroachment pressure, improve tourism, and restore the heritage character. Ask: Has this been included in your manifesto? If elected, will you allocate Smart City or state heritage funds for a pilot heritage lane pedestrianisation within 18 months?
Heritage Urban design
5
Make your 2026 manifesto commitments legally binding — with milestones and public reporting
The sitting MLA made 16 promises in 2021. By his own account 90% are fulfilled — but residents dispute this on key items like the bus terminus and Buckingham Canal. The accountability gap (Lowlight L10) must close. Ask: Will you publish a signed commitment document listing your 2026 promises with specific timelines, and agree to a biannual public report-back through Mylapore's RWAs?
Accountability

📢  How to use this section: Screenshot or print these asks. When a candidate comes to your door, your polling booth, or a public meeting — ask them directly.
